# Career Ladders

> Career ladders are how we tell engineers what "better" looks like — and how we promise that growth does not require leaving engineering or leaving the company. They also force leaders to be specific about what we value.

## Two tracks, equal in dignity

```
Individual Contributor                Management
─────────────────────────             ────────────────────
IC4  Senior Engineer            ──    M3  Engineering Manager
IC5  Staff Engineer             ──    M4  Senior EM / Group EM
IC6  Senior Staff Engineer      ──    M5  Director
IC7  Principal Engineer         ──    M6  Senior Director / VP
IC8  Distinguished Engineer     ──    M7  VP / Head of Engineering
```

- **Equal compensation** at each level. An IC5 and an M3 earn within the same band.
- **Equal access to influence.** Staff+ engineers attend leadership forums.
- **Movement between tracks** is expected and supported. Most VPs I know were Staff engineers first.

## The five dimensions every level is measured on

Every level definition uses the same five dimensions. Levels differ in *scope* and *consistency*, not the dimensions themselves.

| Dimension | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Technical depth** | Quality of the work itself: code, design, judgment under uncertainty |
| **Scope of impact** | Team → multi-team → org → company → industry |
| **Leverage** | How much you make others around you more effective |
| **Ownership** | What you are personally accountable for — and what you would catch if it broke |
| **Communication** | Written, verbal, cross-functional, executive |

## IC ladder, in one line per level

- **IC4 — Senior Engineer.** Owns features end-to-end on a team. Mentors juniors. Trusted to ship without supervision.
- **IC5 — Staff Engineer.** Owns a *system* or a multi-quarter technical bet. Influences team architecture. Multiplies the team's output. The [staff scope template](../templates/staff-engineer-scope-template.md) defines the contract.
- **IC6 — Senior Staff.** Owns a *domain* across multiple teams. Sets technical direction. Mentors other staff engineers.
- **IC7 — Principal.** Owns architectural direction across the engineering org. Often the deciding voice on the ARB. Sometimes externally visible.
- **IC8 — Distinguished.** Industry-level impact. Defines what the company is technically known for. Extremely rare.

## Management ladder, in one line per level

- **M3 — EM.** Owns a team of 5–8 engineers. Coaches, hires, runs the team's operating cadence.
- **M4 — Senior / Group EM.** Owns 2–4 teams via EM reports. Owns a domain's outcomes.
- **M5 — Director.** Owns a function (e.g. Platform) with 30–60 engineers. Sits on the engineering leadership team.
- **M6 — Sr Director.** Owns multiple functions or a large business unit. Builds and develops Directors.
- **M7 — VP / Head of Eng.** Owns the engineering operating system itself. Reports to CEO.

## Promotion process

1. **Manager proposes** with a written packet: scope examples, impact, peer feedback, and which dimension the engineer is being promoted *for*.
2. **Calibration committee** (the engineer's skip-level + 2 peer managers + 1 staff+ engineer) reviews and challenges. Calibration runs **twice a year**, on a published schedule.
3. **Head of Engineering signs** all IC5+/M4+ promotions.
4. **Feedback to the engineer is written** regardless of outcome.

**Anti-patterns:**
- "Time in level" as a sufficient reason. It is necessary, not sufficient.
- Promotion as a retention tool. Comp adjustments are for retention; promotion is for demonstrated scope.
- Surprise denials. If the calibration result will be "no," the engineer should not have been put up.

## Performance, separate from level

We separate three things:

- **Level** — what you are expected to do.
- **Performance** — whether you are doing it (Below / Meeting / Exceeding).
- **Compensation** — a function of both level and performance, plus market.

A high-performing IC5 should out-earn a meeting-expectations IC6 in the lower part of the band. This stops promotion from being the *only* lever for rewarding excellent work.

## AI and the ladder

AI changes what each level can be expected to ship, but not the dimensions. Specifically:

- **IC4–IC5:** AI-assisted productivity is now table stakes. Inability to leverage tooling is a development area.
- **IC5–IC6:** higher leverage from AI means more is expected in *judgment* — when *not* to ship the generated thing.
- **IC6–IC8:** Staff+ engineers are expected to teach, evaluate, and govern AI usage on behalf of their teams. See [AI policy](ai-assisted-development-policy.md).

The bar rises with the tools. The ladder reflects that explicitly each year.
