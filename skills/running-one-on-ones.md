# Running One-on-Ones

> The 1:1 is the smallest unit of management. It is where trust is built or lost, where careers are coached, where small problems are caught before they're big ones. A manager whose 1:1s are status meetings is a manager who doesn't yet manage.

---

## Purpose

You can run a recurring 1:1 with each direct report that they look forward to: their agenda, focused on their growth and obstacles, building trust over time, surfacing what's actually going on, and producing real follow-through.

---

## Why it matters in an AI-first org

- **More work is async; the 1:1 is one of the few reliably-synchronous human moments.** Don't waste it on what a written update would do.
- **Career conversations happen earlier and faster.** Skills become obsolete or differently-valuable on shorter cycles; growth needs more frequent recalibration.
- **AI raises the cost of disengaged-but-still-shipping employees.** They can coast longer before it shows. The 1:1 is where you notice.

---

## The practice

### The structure that works

- **Weekly, 30 minutes**, for most reports. Bi-weekly for senior, mature ICs only with their agreement.
- **Their agenda first, your agenda second.** A shared doc with their topics on top.
- **Standing sections** (light): wins / what's on your mind / blockers / growth / one thing from me. Don't ritualize; do use as a checklist when it stalls.
- **Same time, same place, very rarely cancelled.** Cancelling 1:1s is the loudest "you don't matter" signal in management. Reschedule, don't cancel.
- **Their notes are theirs; your notes are yours.** Shared agenda doc + private manager notes for performance, growth, patterns over time.

### The conversations to make space for

These are the conversations the report won't initiate. *You* have to ask.

- **How is the work?** Not "what are you working on" (that's status). "Is the work satisfying? Is anything frustrating? What's surprising?"
- **How are you?** Energy, sleep, life-outside-work — proportional to the relationship and what they want to share. Don't pry; don't pretend it doesn't exist.
- **What do you need from me?** Asked directly. Most reports won't volunteer this.
- **What's one thing I could do better?** ([giving-and-receiving-feedback.md](giving-and-receiving-feedback.md)). The first three answers will be diplomatic; ask anyway.
- **How are you growing?** Quarterly at least. Tie to the [career ladder](../docs/career-ladders.md). What's the next skill? Who could mentor? What stretch is available?
- **How are you with your team / partners / cross-functional?** Surfaces interpersonal friction early.
- **What's one thing we should change about how we work?** Surfaces process pain you can't see from your seat.

### The conversations *not* to use the 1:1 for

- **Routine status.** That's what async updates / standups are for. A 1:1 spent on status is a 1:1 not spent on growth.
- **Performance reviews.** Use the formal cadence. Don't ambush feedback into a 1:1 once a quarter; give feedback weekly.
- **Tactical problem-solving alone.** If a 30-min 1:1 becomes a working session, fine — but check that you're not avoiding the personal conversations because tactics are easier.
- **Therapy.** Be supportive; refer to actual support (EAP, professional help) when it's beyond your role.

### Tone and behavior

- **Listen 70%, talk 30%.** Most new managers reverse this.
- **Ask questions, don't dispense answers.** Especially for senior reports. Help them think; don't think for them.
- **Take notes; act on them.** A 1:1 that produces no follow-through trains the report not to bring real topics.
- **Close the loop on past topics.** "Last week you said X. What's happening with that?" Signals you actually listened.
- **Sit on the same side of the table.** Walking 1:1s, neutral spaces, video on — choose what makes the report feel collaborated-with, not interrogated.

## Drills

- **Each 1:1 — start with one personal-relationship question.** Build the habit. Most managers default to work too fast.
- **Weekly — review your 1:1 notes** across all reports. What patterns are showing? Whose engagement is drifting? Whose growth is stalled?
- **Monthly — ask each report for 1:1 feedback.** "Is this 30 minutes useful to you? What would make it more useful?" Adjust.
- **Quarterly — career conversation.** Explicit, scheduled, against the [career ladder](../docs/career-ladders.md). Not slipped into a regular 1:1.
- **With your manager:** model the 1:1 you want your reports to want. Bring real topics; expect real engagement. If your own 1:1 is bad, fix that first.
- **For new managers:** sit in on a senior peer's 1:1 (with permission). The patterns are visible only when watched.

## Common failure modes

- **The status 1:1.** 30 minutes of "what'd you ship; what's blocking; what's next." Could have been a Slack message. Wastes the synchronous time.
- **Cancellation by reflex.** "Crazy week — skip ours?" The report hears: "you matter less than my other meetings." Reschedule. Always.
- **The manager monologue.** You talk for 25 of 30 minutes. Now it's your 1:1, not theirs.
- **No notes, no follow-through.** Topics raised; nothing happens; the report stops raising real topics.
- **Skipping the personal entirely.** The work-only 1:1 misses the leading indicators of disengagement, burnout, and life-changes affecting work.
- **Performance reviews by surprise.** First negative feedback the report has heard, delivered at review time, in writing. Process failure.
- **Same 1:1 for every report.** Senior, junior, new, struggling, thriving — they need different conversations. Adapt.
- **AI-summarized 1:1 notes shared with the report.** Use AI for *your private* note-taking if you want; never replace the personal artifact of having paid attention.
- **The 1:1 as your career-tracking dashboard.** They notice. Reports become performative; signal collapses.

---

## Tune for your context

- **New manager, new team:** weekly 30 min, no skipping for 3 months, heavy on relationship-building and listening.
- **Mature senior IC reports:** can flex to 45 min bi-weekly *if they prefer*. Default is still weekly.
- **Skip-level reports:** quarterly is the right cadence for most orgs; agenda is "what's working / not working / things I should know."
- **Distributed teams:** invest in video-on, predictable time-of-day, occasional in-person if possible. The relational deficit needs more, not less, attention.
- **High-stress moments (post-incident, post-reorg, post-departure):** 1:1s temporarily lean toward emotional support and stability. Adjust.
