# Influence & Executive Communication

> Senior leaders rarely fail because of what they know. They fail because of what they couldn't get others to do, or because they couldn't make their thinking land with a CEO, a board, a peer, or 300 engineers in a town hall. Influence is the system that turns judgment into outcomes.

---

## Purpose

You can move decisions, resources, and behavior — across teams you don't manage, across executives who have other priorities, across an organization that has its own inertia — through clarity, credibility, and care. You can communicate with executives in a way that respects their time and earns trust.

---

## Why it matters in an AI-first org

- **AI changes the leverage point.** The work that compounds is increasingly *cross-team* — platform investments, governance, evals, security, FinOps — none of which a single team can land alone. Influence is the mechanism.
- **Senior decisions move faster.** A CTO/CEO who has to wade through fluff to find the ask will not. The communication standard tightens.
- **Trust is the long-term currency.** AI commoditizes execution; it does not commoditize the *track record* of having said what you'd do and done it.

---

## The practice

### The two halves of influence

- **Credibility** — the long-running deposit. Built by consistently doing what you said you'd do, owning your misses, being honest about what you know and don't. It compounds; it cannot be borrowed.
- **Clarity in the moment** — the short-cycle skill. Knowing what you're asking for, why it matters, what you need, and saying it in language and length that fits the audience.

### Executive communication — the load-bearing version

Senior leaders (CEO, board, CFO, CXOs) listen and read differently than your team does. The discipline:

- **BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front.** The first sentence is the ask or the conclusion. Everything after supports it. If they only read line one, they have what they need to act.
- **Time-respect is the protocol.** A 5-minute slot is 5 minutes. A 1-page memo is 1 page. Going over signals you can't prioritize.
- **Anchor to the business.** Tie engineering to revenue, retention, risk, growth, margin. Not to technical elegance.
- **Quantify.** Numbers, ranges, confidence intervals. "p99 dropped 60%, NPS up 4 points, AI inference cost down 35%."
- **Name the decision asked.** "I'm asking you to approve X" or "I'm informing you of Y" or "I want your input on Z." Don't make them guess.
- **Surface the risk you haven't solved**, not just the wins. Hiding the risk loses credibility when it surfaces later. Naming it builds trust.
- **Have the next layer of detail ready.** If they pull a thread, you have the data; if they don't, you don't burn time presenting it.
- **Read the room.** Adjust depth in real time. The most effective communicators end early when the room has heard enough.
- **Follow up in writing.** "Per our conversation: decision X, owner Y, by Z. Open items: A, B." Becomes the record.

### The standing leadership artifacts

- **A regular written update** — weekly or biweekly, to your boss and key partners. Format: top 3 wins, top 3 risks, top 3 asks. Short. Predictable. Searchable.
- **A leadership-team memo for each significant change.** A reorg, a strategy shift, a hiring slowdown, a major incident. Don't surprise your senior peers; brief them before broader communication.
- **The board narrative** if you operate at that level. Quarterly. Same shape every time. Boards reward predictability of *format*; surprises belong in the content.

### Cross-functional influence (your peers, their teams)

- **Make the other side's life easier.** Bring the doc, the proposal, the draft. Don't bring a problem; bring an option.
- **Listen before pitching.** Understand the peer's pressures and goals; tie your ask to those, honestly.
- **Trade openly.** "If we do this, what's on your plate that would benefit from our help?" Reciprocity scales.
- **Don't go around peers to their bosses casually.** It buys this one decision and costs ten. Escalate only when explicitly necessary and with the peer informed.
- **Disagree and commit, visibly.** "I'd have chosen differently; we've decided X; here's how my team will execute it." Models the behavior you want.

### Persuasion craft

- **Earn the right to push.** You've delivered? You can push hard. You haven't? Smaller asks; build the track record.
- **Match the medium to the message.** Hard news in person (or video). Routine update in writing. Don't break bad news in Slack.
- **Tell stories, not just stats.** A specific customer; a specific incident; a specific engineer's experience. Numbers persuade analysts; stories move humans. Use both.
- **Steelman the opposing view.** "I know the argument against this is X; here's why I think we should do it anyway." Disarms the room; signals seriousness.
- **Be specific about what you need.** "I need 2 engineers and a $500k budget for one quarter" beats "I need more resources."
- **Anticipate the second-order question.** "Why now?" "Why this team?" "What happens if we don't?" Have the answer ready.

### Town halls and large-group communication

- **Lead with the news.** Don't bury the change in slide 28. People will read the headline anyway; control it.
- **Acknowledge the hard parts.** Layoffs, missed targets, painful trade-offs. Naming them honestly is more reassuring than spinning them.
- **Q&A is the highest-signal part.** Make it real. Take the hard questions; answer the ones you can; "I don't know yet, here's when I will" for the ones you can't.
- **Repeat the core message.** The seven-times rule is real; you'll be tired of it before the org has heard it.
- **Show up afterward.** Skip-levels, hallway, async questions. Town hall is the start, not the end.

### Working with AI on communication

- **Use AI to pressure-test drafts.** "Argue against this." "What's confusing here?" Useful.
- **Don't outsource the voice.** Your communications are your relationships. AI-shaped exec memos read as exactly what they are.
- **Don't outsource the hard message.** Reorg memos, performance comms, condolences, layoff notes — write them yourself. Always.
- **Use AI for the draft of the routine update**, then heavily edit. The mechanical first pass; your judgment is the deliverable.

## Drills

- **Weekly — write the BLUF first.** For every memo, doc, or update, draft the one-sentence ask or conclusion before anything else. Edit until it holds up alone.
- **Weekly — read a great executive communicator.** Bezos letters, public memos from operators, Buffett shareholder letters, public reorg memos from operators you respect.
- **Monthly — re-read your own communications.** Where did the message land? Where didn't it? Why?
- **Monthly — ask for feedback from your CEO / CTO / boss.** "What's one thing about how I communicate up that I should change?" Most leaders never ask; the signal is sitting there.
- **Quarterly — coach a peer or report on a hard message.** Teaching the discipline sharpens your own.
- **Before any high-stakes meeting:** rehearse the first 60 seconds out loud. The opening sets the rest.

## Common failure modes

- **Bury the lede.** Three slides of context before the ask. The exec has tuned out by slide 2.
- **Tech-speak with non-technical execs.** Words like "sharding," "service mesh," "vector store" without translation. Lost the room; looked junior.
- **No decision asked.** Long doc; ambiguous ending; sits unread.
- **Surprise.** Bad news the senior peer or boss is hearing for the first time in a room. Cardinal sin of executive communication.
- **Spinning a miss.** Treats the audience as not-noticing. They notice; credibility evaporates.
- **Influence-as-politics.** Backchannels, alliance-building, withholding information. Wins this quarter; loses three.
- **No follow-up.** Decisions made verbally and not documented. Re-litigated in 4 weeks.
- **Same message, same depth, every audience.** Junior eng all-hands deck used at board meeting (or vice versa). Insults both.
- **Hiding behind data.** Numbers presented without a recommendation. Senior audience asks "so what?" and you don't have one.
- **AI-drafted, voice-flat communications.** Generic. Hedged. Indistinguishable from any other org's update. Forgettable.

## Tune for your context

- **Engineering leader to CEO/CFO:** business-impact language; numbers; risks; concise asks. Less technical depth; more strategic framing.
- **Engineering leader to board:** standard format, quarterly cadence, no surprises, written. Verbal is summary; written is record.
- **VP to peer VPs:** more horse-trading and shared interest; transparency builds reciprocity over time.
- **VP to engineering org:** more story, more vision, more why; backed by specifics so people can act.
- **Distributed / global org:** invest more in written; verbal moments are scarce and high-stakes.
- **In a crisis:** more frequent communication, even with little new to say; honest about what you know and don't.
- **As you get more senior:** less doing, more communicating; the proportion shifts. Don't resent it; that *is* the job at the top.

- **Chip & Dan Heath** — *Made to Stick* — what makes ideas memorable; SUCCESS framework.
- **Jeff Bezos** — [annual shareholder letters](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/company-news/2021-letter-to-shareholders) — masterclass in executive writing at scale.
- **Warren Buffett** — Berkshire Hathaway annual letters — long-running standard for honest, clear communication to investors.
- **Andy Grove** — *High Output Management* and *Only the Paranoid Survive* — the executive-influence frame from one of the best.
- **Lara Hogan** — *Resilient Management* — practical influence scripts for engineering leaders.
- **Patrick Lencioni** — *The Advantage* and *The Five Dysfunctions of a Team* — peer-leader dynamics.
- **Robert Cialdini** — *Influence* — the underlying psychology; use ethically.
- **William Zinsser** — *On Writing Well* — the executive-writing foundation. Read once a year.
