# Coaching & Growth

> A manager's first job is to make the team better at the work. That's coaching. The 2026 version of it accounts for AI changing what "the work" is — without losing the perennial truth that growth happens through practice, feedback, and stretch.

---

## Purpose

You can help an engineer get measurably better at their craft over months and quarters — through deliberate practice, calibrated feedback, well-chosen stretch assignments, and honest level conversations — and you can recognize when growth has plateaued and have the conversation about it.

---

## Why it matters in an AI-first org

- **What "senior" means is being redefined in real time.** Skills are recombining; some leveling rubrics from 2022 don't describe the work anymore. Coaching has to incorporate the new shape.
- **Junior engineers risk skipping foundational practice** because AI shortcuts the early reps. Without those reps, judgment doesn't develop; the L4→L5 transition becomes harder, not easier.
- **Senior engineers risk plateauing into super-prompters** if coaching doesn't push them into the work AI doesn't do (judgment, design, influence, mentorship).
- **The talent retention battle is increasingly about growth**, not comp. People leave because they stopped getting better.

---

## The practice

### Diagnose where they are

- **Map them honestly to the [career ladder](../docs/career-ladders.md).** Not where you wish they were; where they are.
- **Identify the gap between current level and next.** What 2–3 capabilities, demonstrated repeatedly, would justify the next level? Make this *specific*, not vibes.
- **Distinguish skill vs scope.** Sometimes the engineer is ready and the role isn't. Sometimes the role is bigger than they're ready for. Different interventions.
- **Notice plateaus.** 2+ years at the same level with no growth conversation is a signal — either of unhonest leveling, missing stretch, or a mismatch the engineer also needs to face.

### Build the growth plan

- **2–3 focus areas per cycle**, not 10. More is wishful thinking.
- **Concrete, observable practices** for each — drawn from the relevant skill in this library, from the career ladder, from real project work.
- **Stretch assignment tied to the focus area.** Growth happens through *doing*, with support and feedback. Not through reading or watching.
- **Visible to both of you.** A shared doc; reviewed monthly. Not a single-shot annual artifact.
- **Time-bounded.** "This quarter, lead the design review for X" beats "get better at design."

### The coaching moves

- **Ask, don't tell.** "How would you approach this?" before "do it this way." Especially for senior reports. You're growing their judgment, not their compliance.
- **Pair on the work occasionally.** A 90-minute paired session on a real problem is more developmental than a month of advice.
- **Sponsor, don't just mentor.** *Mentor* is conversation; *sponsor* is putting your reputation on the line to give them an opportunity they'd otherwise not have. Sponsorship is what actually advances careers.
- **Match stretch to readiness.** Too easy: bored. Too hard, with no support: discouraged. The zone is "uncomfortable but reachable."
- **Make space for failure.** Reversible stretches; visible support; postmortem the learning, not the outcome.
- **Mentor toward your own role and beyond.** Most managers underdo this. If nobody on your team could replace you in 12 months, that's a coaching failure.

### The hard conversations

These are the conversations that move someone the most — and that managers most often dodge.

- **"You're not at the bar for your level."** Specific, behavioral, repeated, written. Not a surprise at review time. Followed by a plan.
- **"You're ready for the next level — and here's what we'll do to get the demonstration in front of the right people."** Equally rare; equally important.
- **"You've plateaued and we both know it."** Could be ready-for-different-team, ready-for-different-company, or just a stuck moment. Naming it is the unblocking move.
- **"This role isn't going to be your forever."** Sometimes the kindest, most career-serving thing a manager can say.

### Coaching for AI-era growth specifically

- **Push juniors into the foundational reps.** Reading the codebase, debugging hard, writing from scratch occasionally — even when AI could do it faster. The reps build the judgment they'll need later.
- **Push seniors into the AI-amplified work** — leading larger surface area, mentoring more, owning evals and quality, doing the cross-team coordination AI can't.
- **Make AI usage a coaching topic.** "Show me how you use AI in your work. Where does it accelerate you? Where do you over-trust it?" Most engineers never talk about this with anyone.
- **Track skill-relevance over time.** Quarterly: "is what we're rewarding still the right thing? What new skill should we be hiring/growing for?"

## Drills

- **Each 1:1 — one growth question.** Not every week the same one. Rotate: latest stretch? blocker to growth? what's one skill you want to grow? what's an opportunity you'd want to chase?
- **Monthly — review the growth plan.** Has it moved? What evidence? Update.
- **Quarterly — career conversation.** Explicit, scheduled, ~60 min, against the ladder. Document outcomes.
- **Quarterly — peer calibration.** With other managers: walk through your reports' levels and growth. Find the inconsistencies. Avoids both lenient and harsh local norms.
- **Yearly — succession plan.** For each report: who could grow into this role, what would it take? For yourself: who's growing toward your role? Surfaces both readiness and gaps.
- **Read the skills library yourself.** You can't coach what you don't understand. Pick one skill in this folder per quarter to deepen your own grasp.

## Common failure modes

- **Friend-not-manager.** The relationship is good; the growth has stalled because hard truths never get said. Costly for both of you.
- **Over-correcting in feedback.** Telling junior reports what to do every time, instead of growing their judgment. They get faster at executing your instructions and slower at thinking.
- **No growth plan.** "We'll figure it out." Predictable: nothing happens; year-end review is awkward.
- **Promotion as the only growth signal.** Skills can grow significantly within a level; if your only currency is promotion, you have nothing to give 90% of the time.
- **Stretch without support.** Throwing someone in and watching. Cruelty disguised as opportunity.
- **Support without stretch.** Pleasant 1:1s, real work, no growth. Plateau. Departure in 18 months.
- **Avoiding the level-down conversation** because it's hard. The person knows. Saying it, with care, is the relief.
- **Mentoring instead of sponsoring.** Conversations are cheap; opportunities are scarce. The senior whose only contribution is advice isn't actually advancing anyone.
- **Coaching everyone the same way.** Junior, senior, struggling, thriving — they need different interventions.
- **Treating AI fluency as the new ceiling.** It's a floor, not a ceiling. Growth is about what comes after.

---

## Tune for your context

- **Early-stage / scrappy:** less formal leveling; more "growth via the next chunk of scope." Career conversations still belong; the ladder is lighter.
- **Mature, leveled org:** explicit ladder calibration; peer-review of growth assessments; promotion process with evidence packets.
- **High-IC-density teams** (Staff, Principal heavy): your coaching is more sponsorship and scope-sharing than skill-teaching. Different muscle.
- **High-turnover environment:** invest more in early-relationship growth conversations; the time-to-leverage is shorter.
- **Distributed teams:** scheduling explicit coaching conversations matters more (less ambient mentorship happens).
