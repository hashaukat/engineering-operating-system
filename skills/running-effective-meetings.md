# Running Effective Meetings

> Meetings are the most expensive thing engineers do per minute, and the easiest thing to do badly. The organization that runs them with discipline reclaims hours per person per week; the one that doesn't drowns in calendar-tetris and calls it work.

---

## Purpose

You can decide whether a meeting should happen, design it to produce a specific outcome, run it on time with the right people contributing, and produce a written artifact that makes the meeting durable beyond the call.

---

## Why it matters in an AI-first org

- **Async-default is the norm.** A meeting is now a deliberate choice to spend synchronous time, not a default.
- **Costs are visible.** 30 minutes × 8 people = a workday gone. Meetings that don't earn it are a top-3 productivity tax.
- **AI changes the math.** Notes, summaries, action items can be automated — *if* the meeting was structured enough to be summarizable. A rambling meeting produces a rambling summary.

---

## The practice

### Decide whether the meeting should exist

Before scheduling, answer:

- **What decision will be made or what outcome produced?** If "discussion," it's probably not a meeting yet — write a doc, get async comments, *then* meet if a decision needs convergence.
- **Could a doc + comments produce the same result?** If yes, do that.
- **Is this recurring out of habit?** Every recurring meeting should be killed annually and re-justified. Most fail the test.
- **What's the smallest set of people needed?** Each invitee should be necessary; if they're "kept informed," send notes instead.

### Design the meeting

- **Title says the outcome.** "Decide Q3 platform investments" beats "Platform sync."
- **Agenda in the invite.** Topics, time-box per topic, decision owner. No agenda = no meeting.
- **Pre-read attached.** For any decision meeting, a 1–2 page doc people read *before*. Bezos's 6-pager is the canonical version of this — silent reading at the start ensures it happens.
- **The right people, no more.** Decision-maker + 2–4 contributors + an optional scribe. Crowded meetings produce no decisions.
- **Clear roles:** facilitator (runs it), decision-maker (decides), scribe (writes), contributors (contribute). One person can hold multiple roles; the roles still exist.
- **Right cadence.** Weekly standing meetings should justify themselves every 3 months; many should become bi-weekly or async.

### Run the meeting

- **Start on time. End on time, or early.** Both build the habit. Ending early once builds more goodwill than 10 on-time starts.
- **Restate the outcome at minute one.** "By the end of 30 minutes, we'll have decided X." Resets the room.
- **Drive to decisions.** "What do we need to do in the next 5 minutes to land this?" Beats letting the discussion meander.
- **Park rabbit holes.** Acknowledge, capture, move on. Don't let one issue eat the meeting.
- **Notice who isn't talking.** Pull them in. "Maya, what's your read?" Silence often hides the most important objection.
- **Watch the time per topic.** When you're 5 min over on a 10-min item, decide: more time (cut something else) or move on (park).
- **Land decisions in the room.** "So we've decided X, owner is Y, by Z. Anyone disagree?" Silence in the room is agreement only if you explicitly invited dissent.

### Close the meeting

- **Last 2 minutes: read back the decisions, owners, dates.** This is the artifact. If you can't read it back, you didn't decide.
- **Send the summary within 24 hours.** Decisions, owners, dates, and unresolved items. Short. Searchable. Skim-friendly.
- **Schedule the next checkpoint** if the work continues. Doesn't have to be another meeting.

### Specific meeting types

- **Standups:** 15 min, status only, blockers surfaced. If a topic needs depth, take it offline. Most standups are too long because they devolve into problem-solving.
- **1:1s:** see [running-one-on-ones.md](running-one-on-ones.md).
- **Design reviews:** pre-read mandatory; the room is for objections and decisions; presentation is minimal.
- **Decision meetings:** pre-read + decision owner + tight facilitation; designed to converge.
- **Retros:** time-boxed; structured prompts; action items with owners; not a complaint session.
- **All-hands / town halls:** content has to earn the time; Q&A is the highest-signal part.
- **Brainstorms:** rare; generation work better done async; meet only to *converge*, not to *generate*.

### Using AI in meetings

- **AI note-takers can replace the scribe** for many meetings — *check policy first* re: data, consent, sensitivity.
- **Use AI-generated summaries as a draft.** Always edit before sending; AI summaries miss the decisions and over-quote tangents.
- **Get consent.** Recording / transcription needs explicit opt-in for most contexts.
- **Don't use AI to substitute for paying attention.** "Just read the summary later" trains people to skip meetings they're invited to but don't need to be in. Fix the invite list instead.

## Drills

- **Each meeting you run — close with read-back.** Decisions, owners, dates, said aloud in the last 2 minutes. Build the habit.
- **Weekly — audit your calendar.** Which meetings produced a real decision or outcome? Which were status that could have been async? Kill or restructure two per quarter.
- **Monthly — ask attendees for one improvement.** "What's one thing I could do differently in this meeting?" The signal compounds.
- **Quarterly — kill every recurring meeting and re-justify.** Default: not on the calendar. Only what re-justifies stays.
- **Before any meeting you didn't call:** decide if you actually need to attend. Declining is a skill; "is there a doc instead?" is a kind redirect.
- **Watch a meeting you don't run.** Note: was time used well? Did decisions get made? Bring the lessons back.

## Common failure modes

- **No agenda.** Default outcome: rambling, no decision, vague follow-up. Repeats.
- **No pre-read.** The first 20 minutes is briefing the room. Wasted synchronous time.
- **Too many people.** Every additional invitee multiplies cost and reduces decision-making throughput.
- **Status-as-meeting.** Information that could have been a Slack post burning hours of sync time.
- **No decision owner.** Long discussion; no decision; "let's revisit next week."
- **Recurring meetings that survive their purpose.** They existed for a real reason 18 months ago; that reason is gone; the meeting persists.
- **Running long by default.** Trains everyone to back-to-back schedule and trains presenters to fill the time. Ending early is a kindness.
- **One voice dominates.** The most senior person talks 80%. Now you have one person's opinion validated, not a decision.
- **No follow-up.** Decisions made and forgotten. The meeting was theater.
- **AI summary used as a replacement for the discipline of the meeting.** Garbage in, polished garbage out.
- **The 9am all-team-can-it-be-async meeting.** Tradition that costs an aggregate week per quarter and produces little.

## Tune for your context

- **Distributed / async-first orgs:** lean harder on docs + async; meet to *converge* or *connect*, not to inform.
- **High-trust, small teams:** lighter agendas, more emergent discussion — but still close with decisions and dates.
- **Larger orgs:** more discipline, more standardized formats, more explicit roles. The cost of casual meetings scales.
- **Executive-level meetings:** even tighter pre-reads, even shorter decisions; their time-cost is the highest.
- **Cross-functional / partner meetings:** more upfront alignment work in the invite; the cost of misaligned attendees is high.
- **Creative / discovery work:** different rules; less convergence-pressure, more space; still time-boxed, still with an outcome (option-set, hypothesis, plan).

- **Cal Newport** — *A World Without Email* and *Deep Work* — the broader argument for protecting deep time from meeting sprawl.
