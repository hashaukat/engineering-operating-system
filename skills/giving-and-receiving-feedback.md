# Giving & Receiving Feedback

> Feedback is the mechanism by which an organization gets better. Where feedback is rare, vague, or one-directional, performance drifts and trust erodes. Where it's frequent, specific, and reciprocal, both throughput and retention compound.

---

## Purpose

You can give feedback — to a peer, a report, a manager, or a leader — that is clear, kind, specific, and actionable, in real time, without it becoming a meeting. You can receive feedback without defending, deflecting, or collapsing, and you visibly act on it.

---

## Why it matters in an AI-first org

- The work cycle is faster. Quarterly reviews are too coarse a clock to be the primary feedback loop; the loop has to be daily and weekly.
- AI flattens many surface-level skill differences. The remaining differentiators — judgment, collaboration, taste — are *only* developable through human feedback.
- AI cannot give you feedback on the parts of your work it can't see: how you behave in a room, how you handle ambiguity, how others experience working with you.

## The practice

### Giving feedback

- **Sooner is better.** Same-day beats next-week beats next-quarter. The signal degrades fast.
- **Private for negative, public for positive.** Praise in the open, correct in private. Almost always.
- **Specific behavior, specific impact.** Not "be more collaborative" — "in yesterday's design review, you cut Maya off twice; she went quiet and we didn't hear her objection to the cache choice." Behavior + impact, no character verdict.
- **Use SBI** (Situation–Behavior–Impact) for negative, **SBI-I** (add Intent-check) for harder cases. Cheap, repeatable, hard to mess up.
- **Ask, don't tell, on judgment calls.** "What were you optimizing for?" before "you should have done X."
- **Land it as care, not criticism.** "I'm telling you this because I want you to grow / want this project to land / want you to succeed." The frame changes how it's heard.
- **End with the ask.** "Could you do X next time?" or "What would help you do this differently?" Feedback without an ask is just commentary.
- **Be willing to be wrong.** "Here's how it landed for me — does that match your experience?" Sometimes you missed context.

### Receiving feedback

- **Default response: "Thank you. Let me think about it."** Buys time, prevents defensiveness, signals you take it seriously.
- **Don't argue in the moment.** Even if you disagree. The argument poisons the relationship and the next piece of feedback never comes.
- **Separate the signal from the delivery.** Bad delivery doesn't mean wrong signal. Take the signal; coach the delivery (later, separately).
- **Find the 10% that's true** even in feedback you think is mostly wrong. There's almost always something.
- **Visibly act.** Six weeks later, the giver should see the change. Without that, the feedback loop closes for good.
- **Close the loop.** "I sat with what you said. Here's what I changed." Short. Powerful. Rare.

### Soliciting feedback

- **Ask for it specifically, not generically.** "Was that meeting tight?" beats "any feedback?"
- **Ask in the moment.** "What would have made that better?" right after the thing.
- **Make it cheap.** A 60-second response is more valuable than a scheduled session. Lower the threshold and you'll get more signal.
- **Ask people who don't owe you a kind answer.** Peers and skip-levels often have the highest-leverage view.

## Drills

- **Daily — one piece of feedback given.** Aim for one piece of real, specific feedback (positive or constructive) per day. Track it for a month.
- **Weekly — SBI on a hard one.** Each week, identify one piece of feedback you've been sitting on. Draft it in SBI form. Deliver it.
- **Weekly — one piece of feedback solicited.** Ask someone "what's one thing I could have done better this week?" The first three answers will be diplomatic; the fourth starts to be useful.
- **Monthly — the upward-feedback drill.** Give your manager one specific piece of feedback. Most managers receive almost none and need it most.
- **Quarterly — the 360 lite.** Ask 3–5 colleagues (peers, reports, partners) for one thing to start, stop, continue. Patterns across 3+ are real.
- **With your manager:** practice receiving negative feedback without defending. Ask them to give you the hardest piece they've been holding back.

## Common failure modes

- **The feedback sandwich.** Praise → criticism → praise. Everyone now hears the praise as setup. Skip it; deliver the actual point.
- **Vague niceness.** "Great job!" / "Could be better." Carries zero signal.
- **Saving it for the review.** A piece of feedback delivered three months later has lost almost all its actionability. Now it's just a grievance.
- **Character verdicts.** "You're not strategic enough." Not actionable, deeply demotivating. Behavior + impact, every time.
- **Public negative feedback.** Burns trust in the relationship and intimidates everyone watching.
- **Defensiveness on receipt.** Explaining, justifying, contextualizing in the moment. Even if you're right, you've taught the giver not to bother again.
- **Acting on it once, then drifting back.** The change has to stick to be real.
- **Avoiding it because it's uncomfortable.** This is the most common manager failure. You owe people the truth about how they're showing up.
- **Using AI to draft hard feedback.** It reads as exactly what it is. Write your own.

## Tune for your context

- **High-context cultures** (some regions, some industries) — adjust directness; don't lose specificity.
- **Senior people:** the feedback they get is almost always softer than what they need. Be willing to be the person who tells them the truth.
- **Newer people:** ratio matters; over-index on specific positive feedback for the first months, even when correcting course.
- **Performance management contexts:** feedback that might inform a rating or PIP needs to be *prior, written, and unmistakable*. Surprise is a process failure.
- **Remote / async:** more written, more frequent; in-the-moment voice feedback is harder, so the written discipline matters more.
- **AI-mediated review** (e.g., AI-summarized 360s): treat as input only; the human judgment is the deliverable.

## Further reading

- **Kim Scott** — *Radical Candor* — care personally + challenge directly; the canonical 2×2.
- **Douglas Stone & Sheila Heen** — *Thanks for the Feedback* — receiving feedback as a discipline (the underrated half).
- **Center for Creative Leadership** — [SBI Feedback Model](https://www.ccl.org/articles/leading-effectively-articles/closing-the-gap-between-intent-vs-impact-sbii/) — the standard structured format.
- **Marshall Goldsmith** — *What Got You Here Won't Get You There* — feedback patterns at senior levels; "feedforward" technique.
- **Lara Hogan** — [larahogan.me](https://larahogan.me/) — practical scripts and templates, especially for managers.
- **Patty McCord** — *Powerful* — high-frequency, high-honesty feedback as a Netflix-style cultural baseline.
