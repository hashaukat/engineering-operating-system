# Hiring & Interviewing

> Hiring is the highest-leverage decision a manager makes. A great hire compounds for years; a bad hire compounds in the opposite direction — on the team, the work, and your time. In an AI-first org, the bar moves *and* the signals you read for change.

This is the personal-skill companion to [hiring-process.md](../docs/hiring-process.md).

---

## Purpose

You can run a fair, calibrated, time-respectful interview loop that reliably distinguishes candidates who will succeed in the role from those who won't, in a labor market where AI fluency, judgment, and collaboration are the differentiators.

---

## Why it matters in an AI-first org

- **AI fluency is a real, observable skill.** Interviews that don't test it hire for the 2018 job.
- **Take-home assignments are now contaminated** by trivial AI use. The signal moved to in-conversation reasoning.
- **Resume signal is weaker.** Generated, polished, sometimes AI-co-authored. Calibration on the loop matters more.
- **The cost of a wrong hire scales with AI-amplified velocity.** A senior who can't think will ship more wrong things, faster.
- **The cost of a wrong rejection scales too.** Strong candidates with non-standard backgrounds are easier to misjudge if your loop is shallow.

## The practice

### Before the loop — design

- **Write the role spec** like a [feature spec](writing-specs.md): user (your team), goals, non-goals, success criteria at 6 and 12 months. The interview tests *against* this.
- **Map the loop to the spec.** Each interview tests one or two specific competencies. No two interviews testing the same thing. No competency untested.
- **Calibrate the bar.** What does "hire" look like at this level for this skill? Write it down. Share with interviewers.
- **Pick interviewers deliberately.** Variety of backgrounds, perspectives, levels. Avoid all-senior-male / all-same-team loops.

### As an interviewer — preparation

- **Read the resume; prepare 2–3 anchored questions.** Generic questions get generic answers.
- **Know what *you* are evaluating** in this interview. Stay in your lane; trust peers in theirs.
- **Prepare a rubric** with concrete signals for hire / lean-hire / lean-no-hire / no-hire. Vague bars produce vague feedback.

### During the interview — conduct

- **Set expectations in 60 seconds.** Format, time, what they'll be evaluated on, when they can ask questions.
- **Make them comfortable enough to do their best work.** Hostile interviews don't measure ability; they measure stress tolerance. (You may want that signal; usually you don't.)
- **Probe depth, not breadth.** "Tell me more about X" 3 times beats jumping to new topics. The third "tell me more" is where the real signal lives.
- **Ask for examples, not opinions.** "Tell me about a time you…" beats "what do you think about…"
- **Look for what they *did*, not what their team did.** "I" vs "we" matters. Probe both.
- **Test reasoning live**, not memorization. The right question lets them think out loud; you evaluate the thinking, not the answer.
- **Be willing to be wrong.** If your first impression is "strong" or "weak," spend the second half deliberately trying to disconfirm it.
- **Leave time for their questions.** *What they ask* is often the strongest signal in the loop.

### Testing AI fluency (a new module)

- **Include at least one interview** that observes how they work *with* AI. Watch them use it; ask what they trust and don't; ask how they verified the output.
- **Equally, include at least one interview without AI.** Foundational reasoning, design, debugging — done on a whiteboard, paper, or shared editor with tools off. This is no longer the whole loop, but it is still part of it.
- **Probe their verification practice.** "Walk me through a time AI gave you something plausible-but-wrong. How did you catch it?" Strong candidates have stories. Weak ones don't.
- **Calibrate expectations to level.** Junior: developing judgment, willing to verify. Senior: explicit practice, can teach others.

### Writing the feedback

- **Same day.** While the conversation is fresh.
- **Specific behavioral evidence**, mapped to rubric: "When asked X, candidate Y, which suggests Z about competency W."
- **One bottom-line recommendation** with confidence: strong hire / hire / lean-hire / lean-no-hire / no-hire / strong-no-hire.
- **No coordinating with other interviewers before writing.** Independent assessments first, then debrief — to avoid groupthink.

### The debrief

- **Read all written feedback first.** Then discuss.
- **Disagreement is a feature, not a bug.** Resolve it by going back to the evidence, not by averaging the opinions.
- **The bar raiser / hiring committee / Approver decides** ([hiring-process.md](../docs/hiring-process.md)). Hiring managers desperate to fill should not solely decide.

### The candidate experience

- **Respond fast.** Decisions in 48 hours, communicated in 24 more. Latency loses candidates and signals chaos.
- **Be honest about the no.** Brief, kind, real. "Not enough signal on X for this role at this time" beats radio silence.
- **Make every candidate, hired or not, want to recommend the loop.** Recruiting is a long game; their next colleague is your next pipeline.

## Drills

- **Each interview — write feedback the same day.** Track for a quarter how often you're late. Late feedback is the most-cited interviewer failure.
- **Monthly — calibrate with a peer.** Compare written feedback on the same candidate. Where do you disagree? Why? Adjust.
- **Quarterly — review hire outcomes.** Did the people you said "hire" on succeed? Did the people you said "no" on go elsewhere and shine? Your loop-quality metric.
- **Sit second-chair.** Shadow a senior interviewer; have a senior interviewer shadow you. Patterns are only visible from the outside.
- **Practice a hard scenario.** "Strong candidate, but a teammate flags concern about collaboration." Walk through what you'd do.
- **Read your own rubric quarterly.** Does it still match what the role needs? Update.

## Common failure modes

- **Same loop for every role.** Junior, senior, IC, manager, specialist — the loop needs to actually test what matters for the role. Reusing IC-loops for managers is a top hiring mistake.
- **Hiring on culture-fit.** Hires people like the existing team; locks in monoculture; loses optionality. Test for *culture-add* instead — what does this candidate bring we don't have?
- **Trick questions / gotchas.** Measures preparation for trick questions; rarely measures the job.
- **Whiteboard puzzles disconnected from the work.** Test for trivia or anxiety tolerance, not for what they'll actually do.
- **Single-interviewer veto without evidence.** "I just got a bad feeling." Unfalsifiable; often reflects bias; corrosive when allowed.
- **Yes-bias under time pressure.** A panel desperate to fill lowers its bar. Predictable. Recoverable only if a bar raiser has actual authority to say no ([hiring-process.md](../docs/hiring-process.md)).
- **No-bias by default.** Some loops reject everyone. If your hire rate is < 5%, your loop is broken or your pipeline is broken; both your problem.
- **AI fluency tested as gimmick** ("write a prompt"). The skill is verification, taste, when not to use it. Test for that.
- **AI fluency not tested at all.** Hired for the 2018 job.
- **Decision in the debrief, not in the written feedback.** The hire happens by social pressure; the written rationale gets reverse-engineered. Hard to audit; hard to learn from.
- **Slow loop.** 4 weeks from first conversation to offer. Strong candidates take other offers; trust evaporates.
- **Reference checks skipped.** The single most signal-rich, cheapest step. Always do them for senior roles.

## Tune for your context

- **Earlier-stage:** smaller loop, more flexible, more candidate-experience-investment per hire; bar can be higher because you're a tighter team.
- **Mature org:** more standardized loop, more calibrated rubric, more reliance on bar raisers / hiring committees.
- **High-volume hiring:** invest in interviewer training; consistency across many loops is the lever.
- **Senior / leadership hiring:** longer reference-check tail; more stakeholder interviews; more time on judgment and trade-off signal than on craft demonstration.
- **Distributed / global:** make the loop accessible across time zones; consider asynchronous components carefully (with AI contamination risk in mind).
- **AI-engineering / AI-research hiring:** specialized loop; calibrate against the moving field; expect to redesign every 6 months.
